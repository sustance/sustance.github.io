For those who choose to build rather than conquer CHAPTER 1: Recruitment Elena - Silicon Valley, January 2038 The conference room had floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking the Santa Clara Valley, but Elena Moss couldn't appreciate the view. She was too busy watching her company disappear. "The acquisition is finalized," said Richard Hastings, Atlantic Aerospace's VP of Emerging Technologies. His smile was professionally warm, the kind practiced in a thousand boardrooms. "BioFabrik is now a wholly owned subsidiary of the Atlantic Consortium. Your bacterial engineering patents are classified as critical national security assets." Elena's fingers found the burn scar on her left forearm, tracing the ridge of tissue. An old habit when stressed. The scar was from her first major lab accident at MIT—the moment she'd learned that synthetic biology could bite back if you weren't careful. "National security," she repeated. The words tasted like ash. "Your extremophile research has applications far beyond industrial processes, Dr. Moss." Hastings pulled up a holographic display. Mars rotated in the air between them, red and dusty and very far away. "Your bacterial strains can process fuel from Martian atmosphere. Extract minerals from regolith. Sustain long-duration settlement. You've built the foundation for energy independence on Mars." "I built them for terrestrial applications. Contaminated site remediation. Biofuel production in extreme climates." "Yes. And now they'll do something more important." Hastings zoomed the display to show a cluster of habitats on Mars's surface. "The American Mars Settlement Initiative needs biological life support. You're the best extremophile engineer in the Western hemisphere. Your bacteria are already adapted for low-pressure, high-radiation environments." Elena felt the walls closing in. She'd started BioFabrik five years ago with venture funding and a vision of making industrial biotechnology sustainable. Now venture firms had sold to aerospace, aerospace answered to governments, and governments had decided her work belonged on another planet. "I'm not a Mars colonist," she said carefully. "I'm a researcher. My lab is here." "Your lab is wherever we need it." Hastings's smile didn't waver, but his tone shifted. "I'm not asking, Dr. Moss. Check your employment contract, section 7, subsection C. 'Employee agrees to relocate as necessary for projects deemed critical to national security.' The legal team assured me you signed it." She had. God, she'd signed so many documents during the acquisition. Barely read half of them, focused on maintaining her research autonomy, protecting her team's jobs. She hadn't imagined... "You're telling me I'm going to Mars." "Eighteen months. Initial deployment. If the mission succeeds, you'll have the option to extend or rotate back to Earth." Hastings closed the hologram. "Transportation leaves from Houston in three months. You'll have full access to resources, unlimited research budget, and the opportunity to enable permanent human settlement of another planet. Most scientists would consider that a career pinnacle." Most scientists hadn't built their own company from scratch, only to watch it become a tool for geopolitical competition. "What if I refuse?" "Then you'll be in breach of contract. BioFabrik's IP reverts fully to Atlantic Consortium. Your team—all fifteen researchers you fought to protect during acquisition—will be reassigned or terminated. And you'll spend the next decade in litigation." He stood, extending his hand. "Or you can go to Mars, do groundbreaking work, and return a hero. Your choice." Elena didn't take his hand immediately. She looked past him at the valley below, the sprawl of tech campuses and startup dreams. She'd climbed out of a working-class Minneapolis neighborhood through scholarships and seventy-hour work weeks. Built something real, something that mattered. And now it belonged to people who saw it as a tool for resource extraction on a planet 140 million miles away. She stood and shook Hastings's hand. His grip was firm, satisfied. "When do I brief my team?" she asked. "They already know. We informed them this morning. They're proud of you, Dr. Moss. You should be too." After he left, Elena stood alone in the conference room, staring at her reflection in the window. Thirty-eight years old. Dark blonde hair escaping its ponytail. Green eyes that looked tired even when she'd slept. The burn scar visible on her forearm, a reminder that biology didn't care about your intentions. She pulled out her phone and opened the message from her ex-husband: Heard about Mars. That's incredible. You'll do amazing things there. David had always been supportive. That's why the divorce had been so amicable—they'd simply wanted different things. He wanted stability, a family, roots. She'd wanted to build something that might outlast her. Looked like she was getting her wish. Just not the way she'd imagined. Elena tied her ponytail tighter and headed back to her lab. She had three months to prepare her bacteria for Mars. Whether she was ready or not. Kenji - Kyoto, January 2038 The tea was cooling in Kenji Sato's hands, but he couldn't bring himself to drink it. He sat in the traditional room of his family's house in Kyoto, looking at the small garden his grandmother had maintained for forty years. Moss grew on the stones. A maple tree caught the winter light. Everything here was about patience, cultivation, respect for what grew slowly. Mars would be the opposite of that. "You're thinking too hard," his mother said from the doorway. "You'll burn a hole in the tatami with your thoughts." Kenji adjusted his glasses—a nervous habit, even though they were already perfectly positioned. "The Ministry called again. They want an answer by tomorrow." His mother entered and knelt across from him with the fluid grace of someone who'd performed the tea ceremony a thousand times. "What does your heart say?" "That this is politics masquerading as science. The Asian BioArchitecture Consortium has developed elegant fungal construction materials. The Ministry wants to deploy them on Mars not because the research is ready, but because Western biotech companies are ahead of us. It's competition disguised as exploration." If I refuse, they'll send someone who cares less about the research and more about beating the Americans. The work will suffer." "So you'll go to protect the work." "Maybe. Or maybe I'm rationalizing because part of me wants to go." He looked at his mother. "Is that selfish?" "Is it selfish to want to build something that's never been built before?" She poured fresh tea into his cup. "Your father built temples, Kenji. Sacred spaces that will stand for centuries. You have a chance to build on another planet. That's not selfish. That's legacy." "Father built for Japan. This Mars project—it's tangled up in national pride, resource competition, all the things I went into science to escape." "You can't escape human nature by changing planets." "The Green Layer will go to Mars regardless. The question is whether it goes with someone who cares about doing it right." There it was. The appeal to responsibility. If the biology requires changing timelines, I change timelines." "Within reason—" "No. Complete autonomy or I refuse. "I'll call you back in two hours," Tanaka said, and disconnected. Kenji set down his tablet and returned to the tea, now cold. He drank it anyway, appreciating the slight bitterness. His phone buzzed. A message from his ex-partner, Hiroshi: Heard you're considering Mars. That's very you—choosing impossible projects over stable relationships. It was meant to sting, and it did. They'd ended things two years ago when Kenji had refused to commit to settling down. Hiroshi had wanted marriage, children, a life built on Earth. Kenji had wanted... What? To build fungal networks on another planet, apparently. He typed back: Some relationships are worth the distance. We weren't one of them. I'm sorry. Then deleted it unsent. What was the point? His mother returned with fresh tea. "You'll go," she said. It wasn't a question. "Probably. If they agree to my terms." "They will. You gave them no choice." She poured for both of them. "Write to me from Mars. Tell me about the growing things there. I want to know if you can make moss grow on Martian stones." "There are no stones on Mars. Just regolith—pulverized rock." "Then you'll have to build the stones first, won't you?" Tanaka called back ninety minutes later. Full research autonomy granted, in writing, signed by the Minister herself. Kenji accepted. That night, he practiced tea ceremony alone in the traditional room, muscle memory guiding his movements. Pour, wait, observe. Patience in every gesture. Respect for the process. He would bring that to Mars. Not the competition, not the urgency, not the political maneuvering. Just the quiet attention to what was growing, and the patience to let it grow right. Even if it took longer than anyone wanted. Even if it cost him everything he was leaving behind. Forty-seven people from six different programs, all converging for three months of Mars prep before launch. Through official channels and scheduled coordination meetings." Torres's tone suggested the conversation was over. "Any other questions?" No one raised their hand. The training was intense. EVA suit certification. Emergency protocols. Habitat maintenance. Medical procedures for environments where the nearest hospital was 140 million miles away. CHAPTER 2: Mars Arrival The transit took three months. Elena spent most of it in her quarters reviewing bacterial cultures, running simulations, and trying not to think about the fact that she was trapped in a metal can hurtling through vacuum at 80,000 kilometers per hour. The day before Mars arrival, Commander Torres gathered everyone in the common area. "Tomorrow you'll see your new home," she said, pulling up real-time footage of Mars. The planet filled the viewscreen, rust-colored and scarred with ancient impacts. "It's not Earth. It's not comfortable. It's not forgiving. But it's humanity's first step toward being multiplanetary, and you're part of that. Remember that when things get hard." The landing was rougher than Elena expected. The transport shuddered and groaned as it hit atmosphere, heat shields glowing. Then the engines kicked in for final descent, and suddenly they were down, dust billowing around the viewports. Welcome to Mars. The airlock sequence took twenty minutes. Pressure equalization, contamination protocols, safety checks. Elena's first steps on Mars were in a full EVA suit, her first breath Martian air filtered through recyclers. Jezero Crater stretched out around them—rust-colored regolith, rocky outcrops, distant crater rim. The sky was butterscotch pink, the sun smaller than on Earth. Everything looked simultaneously alien and oddly familiar, like a desert that had forgotten what water meant. "Beautiful, isn't it?" Sarah Okafor said over the suit radio. Elena wasn't sure beautiful was the right word. Stark. Austere. Uncompromising. "It's something," Elena replied. The American base was a cluster of cylindrical habitats half-buried in regolith for radiation shielding. Solar arrays stretched across several hectares. A greenhouse dome gleamed in the thin sunlight. Everything was functional, industrial, designed for survival rather than comfort. Torres led them through the main airlock into the primary habitat. The interior was warmer, pressurized to Earth-normal, smelling faintly of recycled air and disinfectant. Narrow corridors, efficient spaces, everything bolted down. "Quarters are shared," Torres said. "Two to a room. Communal facilities. Work schedules posted daily. You'll be busy." Elena's quarters were a three-meter by four-meter space with two bunks, storage lockers, and a small desk. Her roommate was a hydroponics engineer from California who introduced herself as Chen Li and immediately claimed the lower bunk. "Hope you don't snore," Chen Li said cheerfully. "Hope you don't either." That night, Elena lay in her bunk listening to the habitat's mechanical sounds. Life support humming. Ventilation cycling. Distant voices from other modules. Outside, Martian night would be hitting -80°C. Inside, they maintained a comfortable 20°C through constant energy expenditure. She thought about her bacteria in the cryogenic storage, waiting to be deployed. Thought about Kenji Sato at the Asian base eight kilometers away, probably thinking about his fungal cultures. Thought about the fact that they were neighbors but forbidden from cooperating. The absurdity of it hit her then. They were on Mars—millions of kilometers from Earth, facing existential challenges that didn't care about national borders—and they were still playing geopolitical games. That had to change. She just didn't know how yet. The Work Begins Elena's first week was orientation. Torres walked new arrivals through base operations, emergency protocols, radiation safety, and the absolutely critical importance of following procedures. "Mars doesn't care if you're having a bad day," Torres said during one briefing. "Forget to check your suit seal, you die. Contaminate the water recycling, people die. Lose focus during a dust storm, everyone dies. There's no margin for error here. We follow procedures or we fail." The Utility Swarm facility was on the eastern edge of the base—a sealed laboratory where Elena's bacterial cultures would be cultivated, tested, and eventually deployed. The equipment was state-of-the-art, shipped from Earth at enormous expense. Sarah Okafor showed her around. "We've got climate-controlled growth chambers, genomic sequencing, metabolic analysis, and enough substrate to culture bacteria for six months. After that, we need to be producing our own growth medium from Martian resources." Elena examined the setup. "Where's the regolith processing station?" "Module C. We extract trace minerals, synthesize basic nutrients, recycle everything. Your bacteria will need to adapt to whatever we can provide locally." "They're extremophiles. They'll adapt or die." Elena pulled up her bacterial strain catalog. "I've got forty-seven variants optimized for different metabolic pathways. We'll run parallel cultures and see which ones thrive." "Darwinian selection on Mars. I like it." Over the next month, Elena established her bacterial colonies in carefully controlled conditions. She started with the strains most likely to succeed—methane-producing bacteria that could synthesize fuel from CO₂ and trace hydrogen, metal-reducing bacteria that could process Martian regolith, and photosynthetic cyanobacteria that could produce oxygen. The results were... mixed. Some strains thrived beyond expectations. The methane-producers adapted quickly, their metabolism accelerating in the low-pressure environment. Within two weeks, they were producing fuel at rates 30% higher than Earth-based projections. Other strains struggled. The metal-reducing bacteria that were supposed to extract minerals from regolith kept dying. The cultures would establish, show promise, then collapse after a few generations. Elena ran diagnostics obsessively, trying to understand what was failing. "Substrate," she muttered one evening, staring at dead bacterial films under the microscope. "They need something to anchor to. Something structural." Sarah looked over her shoulder. "What kind of structure?" "Ideally, a porous matrix with high surface area. Something that holds moisture and provides nutrient flow. On Earth, I used synthetic foams. Here..." Elena pulled up a materials database. "We don't have the right materials. Everything is too dense or breaks down under bacterial metabolism." "What about biological substrate? Something that could grow with the bacteria?" "Like what?" Sarah shrugged. "I don't know. I'm just thinking out loud." Elena filed that thought away. Biological substrate. Something alive that bacteria could colonize. It sounded familiar. She'd read research about fungal-bacterial symbioses. Mycelial networks providing structure while bacteria provided metabolic processing. She'd read it in papers by Kenji Sato. Eight kilometers away at the Asian base, Kenji was probably dealing with the exact opposite problem—fungal structures that needed better nutrient processing. The pieces existed. They were just on opposite sides of an artificial boundary. Kenji - Asian Base, Utopia Planitia Kenji Sato's first sight of Mars was through the viewport of the Asian Transport Helio-7 as it descended toward Utopia Planitia. The landing site was a flat plain of rust-colored regolith, ancient and undisturbed except for their base. The Asian Mars Settlement—officially called Harmony Station—was architecturally different from its American counterpart. Where the American base was industrial and efficient, Harmony Station incorporated aesthetic principles. The habitats were arranged in a mandala pattern, with communal spaces at the center and private quarters radiating outward. Gardens grew under transparent domes. The mess hall featured traditional design elements from multiple Asian cultures. It was an attempt to bring Earth's beauty to Mars. Kenji appreciated the intention while recognizing the challenge—beauty required resources, and resources on Mars were precious. Coordinator Yuki Tanaka welcomed the new arrivals with a formal speech that emphasized collective harmony, shared purpose, and the historic significance of their work. Kenji listened politely while mentally cataloging the base's layout and planning his laboratory space. The Green Layer cultivation facility was impressive. Large climate-controlled domes where fungal networks could be grown in simulated Martian conditions. Genomic analysis equipment for monitoring organism adaptation. And perhaps most importantly—artistic spaces where researchers could sketch and photograph the growing structures, recognizing that biological architecture was as much art as engineering. Dr. Park Min-jun, the Korean botanist who managed the agricultural domes, gave Kenji the tour. "We're growing food successfully," Park said, gesturing at rows of hydroponic vegetables. "Lettuce, tomatoes, soybeans, rice. The Green Layer helps—fungal networks stabilize humidity and moderate temperature fluctuations. But we're still dependent on Earth for seed stock and nutrient supplements." "The goal is Mars-native production?" "Eventually. But the timeline is aggressive. The Ministry wants results faster than biology allows." Park glanced around to ensure they weren't overheard. "Between us, the pressure to demonstrate success is intense. Every report to Earth gets scrutinized for evidence we're ahead of Western programs." Kenji had expected this but still found it frustrating. "Science doesn't work on political timelines." "Tell that to the people funding us." Kenji began his work systematically. He cultivated fungal spores in growth chambers, exposing them to increasingly Martian conditions—low pressure, low temperature, high radiation, minimal moisture. The goal was to evolve strains that could grow in actual Martian regolith while producing structural materials suitable for habitat construction. The fungal cultures were beautiful. Mycelial networks spread through substrate like living lace, white filaments branching with mathematical precision. As they matured, the networks produced chitin-based materials that hardened into foam-like structures—lightweight, insulating, and remarkably strong. But there was a problem. The fungi struggled to extract nutrients from pure Martian regolith. They could grow in enriched substrate—soil brought from Earth, enhanced with organic matter—but that was unsustainable. For the Green Layer to work at scale, it needed to thrive on Mars-native materials. Kenji ran tests obsessively. Different fungal species. Different growth conditions. Different substrate preparation. The fungi always hit the same wall: nutrient limitation. They needed more efficient extraction mechanisms than they'd evolved on Earth. "What if we inoculate with bacterial colonies?" suggested one of the research assistants, a young woman from Thailand named Siriporn who was doing her doctorate on extremophile fungi. "Mycorrhizal relationships?" Kenji considered it. "That's how many Earth plants access nutrients—fungal networks partnered with bacteria that process minerals." "Exactly. We could engineer a Mars-adapted version." It was a good idea. But engineering bacterial partners would require expertise Kenji didn't have. He thought about Elena Moss at the American base.
